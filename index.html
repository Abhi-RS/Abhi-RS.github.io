About Me

I hold a Ph.D. from the School of Computer and Information Sciences (SCIS), University of Hyderabad, completed under the guidance of Prof. P. S. V. S. Sai Prasad. My research lies at the intersection of Rough Set Theory, Knowledge Discovery in Data, and Ensemble Learning, with a particular focus on feature subset selection and diversity-driven classification models. The first strand of my work addressed Optimal and Near-Optimal Reduct Computation, where I designed approaches based on coarsest granularity principles to identify the most informative subsets of features for single classifier systems. The second strand concentrated on Diversity Criteria for Multiple Reducts, developing generalized methods to generate diverse reduct sets and integrate them into ensemble learning frameworks for enhanced predictive performance.

Optimal Reduct Computation: Impact on KDD

In Knowledge Discovery in Data (KDD), the quality of extracted knowledge is strongly influenced by the choice of features used for model building. My research on Optimal Reduct Computation within the framework of Rough Set Theory directly addresses this challenge by identifying the smallest yet most information-rich subsets of attributes, known as reducts. By designing coarsest granularityâ€“based methods for optimal and near-optimal reduct selection, my work ensures minimal redundancy, maximal preservation of classification power, and reduced computational overhead. This not only enhances interpretability of the discovered knowledge but also accelerates data preprocessing, making large-scale and high-dimensional datasets more manageable. As a result, the proposed approaches contribute to more efficient, scalable, and accurate KDD pipelines, enabling robust decision-making in diverse real-world applications.

Diversity in Rough Set based Ensemble Learning

The diversity in multiple reducts computation explores how varying feature subsets derived from Rough Set Theory can be strategically leveraged to enhance the performance of ensemble learning models. Traditional reduct computation focuses on finding a minimal or near-minimal subset of attributes for classification, but often produces similar or overlapping reducts, limiting ensemble diversity and thus the overall predictive power. In my work, I developed generalized diversity heuristics that systematically generate multiple, diverse reducts to ensure each base classifier in an ensemble learns from distinct yet relevant perspectives of the data. This approach not only improves classification accuracy but also increases robustness against overfitting and noise. Furthermore, I proposed scalable methods, including an efficient computation of the Absorbed Discernibility Matrix (ADM) without storing the complete Discernibility Matrix, enabling the application of diversity-driven multiple reducts computation to large-scale, high-dimensional datasets in real-world settings.
